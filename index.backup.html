HYPERGAMBLING RESEARCH
The house edge on a standard American roulette wheel is 5.26%. Every spin is independent. The wheel does not remember what happened before. Yet players keep notebooks full of previous results, convinced the universe owes them a pattern.
In 1873, Joseph Jagger hired six clerks to record the outcomes of roulette wheels at Monte Carlo. He found a biased wheel and won the equivalent of $7 million before the casino caught on and shuffled the wheels nightly.
A martingale system doubles the bet after every loss. In theory it guarantees profit. In practice it guarantees ruin. The table maximum exists precisely because casinos understood this centuries ago.

The Kelly Criterion was published in 1956 by J.L. Kelly Jr. at Bell Labs. It was designed to optimize signal noise over telephone lines. Gamblers repurposed it to size bets. The formula is elegant. The discipline required to follow it is not.
Slot machines in Nevada are required by law to return at least 75% of what they take in. Most return between 85% and 98%. The remaining percentage is the slow, invisible tax on hope.
Edward Thorp beat blackjack with math in 1962 and published the proof for everyone to read. The casinos responded not by closing but by adding more decks. The game adapted. The odds shifted back. The house always evolves.
Expected value is negative in almost every casino game. The exceptions are rare and temporary: mispriced lines, biased wheels, vulnerable shuffle procedures. By the time you find one, a hundred others already have.
The gambler's fallacy is the belief that past outcomes influence future independent events. The hot hand fallacy is the belief that streaks are real. Both are wrong. Both feel absolutely correct when you are at the table.
Casinos pump oxygen, remove clocks, carpet the floors in patterns that discourage lingering in walkways, and serve free drinks. The architecture is not designed for comfort. It is designed for continuation. Every surface says: keep playing.
